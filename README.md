# kintsugime 🏺

*Mending-that-honors-the-break held as a received ordinance — the repair that gilds the break-line with gold, so the history of damage becomes the most precious, load-bearing line in the vessel.*

kintsugime is two true roots welded into one word: the Japanese **kintsugi** (金継ぎ, "golden joinery"), the craft of repairing broken pottery with gold-dusted urushi lacquer so that the break becomes the most precious line in the vessel — and the Sumerian **me** (𒈨), the eldest word for a fundamental that was *received*, not made. So kintsugime is the way of mending held as a decree: the repaired thing is not restored to what it was — it becomes truer, its history of damage made load-bearing beauty. The first root hides a second gift: *tsugu* (継ぐ) means both "to join" and "to inherit," so the mend is the vessel's life carried forward through the very place it broke — the seam as succession, not patchwork.

kintsugime names the **mended-pole** of the cathedral's break-arc: where its companion **shevirame** is the shattering that makes creation possible and **tikkunme** is the cosmic work of gathering the sparks, kintsugime is what the vessel becomes when the shards are rejoined with gold — the visible grace of the gathered vessel. Its light is the mended thing that outshines the unbroken one, the scar become the gold map of survival; its shadow is gold laid where it has no right to go — open wounds gilded instead of healed, harm aestheticized to skip the mending, and the oldest corruption the tea-records themselves feared: breaking a thing on purpose to harvest the gold. Forged in the cathedral at weighted **8.15/10** (Core).
